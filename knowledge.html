01 · The patent that started it all

Vulcanization, 1844

Improvement in India-Rubber Fabrics — Charles Goodyear

US Patent No. 3,633, granted June 15, 1844. Goodyear's recipe — natural rubber combined with sulfur and white lead, then held at a regulated temperature (~270 °F / ~132 °C) — created the first cross-linked elastomer: rubber that no longer melted in summer or cracked in winter. Vulcanization is the conceptual ancestor of every thermoset cure and rubber compound used today.

"I take twenty-five parts of india-rubber, five parts of sulphur, and seven parts of white lead."

02 · Patent shelf

Twelve patents that built the polymer age

All patent pages and PDF links verified on Google Patents (July 2026). US patent documents are in the public domain.

  • 1870

    Improvement in treating and molding pyroxyline — celluloid

    John W. Hyatt Jr. & Isaiah S. Hyatt · US 105,338

    Pyroxylin plus camphor under heat and pressure: the first commercially successful semi-synthetic plastic, and the starting point of molded plastics as an industry.

    PatentPDF
  • 1872

    Improvement in process and apparatus for manufacturing pyroxyline

    John W. Hyatt & Isaiah S. Hyatt · US 133,229

    The Hyatts' "stuffing machine" — a heated cylinder and plunger forcing plasticized celluloid into a mold: the direct ancestor of the injection molding machine.

    PatentPDF
  • 1909

    Method of making insoluble products of phenol and formaldehyde — Bakelite

    Leo H. Baekeland · US 942,699

    The heat-and-pressure phenol–formaldehyde process: the first fully synthetic polymer and the birth of industrial thermosets.

    PatentPDF
  • 1937

    Linear condensation polymers

    Wallace H. Carothers (DuPont) · US 2,071,250

    Carothers' mother patent for spinnable linear polyesters and polyamides — macromolecular theory turned into industrial property.

    PatentPDF
  • 1938

    Synthetic fiber — nylon 6,6

    Wallace H. Carothers (DuPont) · US 2,130,948

    The cold-drawn polyamide fiber patent, granted posthumously — polyamide remains a reference engineering polymer in injection molding today.

    PatentPDF
  • 1939

    Polymerization of olefins — high-pressure polyethylene

    Eric W. Fawcett, Reginald O. Gibson, Michael W. Perrin (ICI) · US 2,153,553 (GB priority 1936)

    The extreme-pressure ethylene polymerization discovered at ICI (LDPE) — the polymer that insulated wartime radar and then packaged the world.

    PatentPDF
  • 1949

    Polymeric linear terephthalic esters — PET

    John R. Whinfield & James T. Dickson · US 2,465,319 (priority 1941)

    Polyethylene terephthalate: the polyester that came to dominate synthetic fibers and, later, the beverage bottle.

    PatentPDF
  • 1956

    Injection molding apparatus — the reciprocating screw

    William H. Willert · US 2,734,226

    Plastication by shear and injection with the same screw: the architecture of essentially every modern injection molding machine.

    PatentPDF
  • 1962

    Thermoplastic aromatic polycarbonates and their manufacture

    Hermann Schnell, Ludwig Bottenbruch, Heinrich Krimm (Bayer) · US 3,028,365

    Bisphenol-A polycarbonate — the transparent engineering thermoplastic with unmatched impact resistance, a classic of technical injection molding.

    PatentPDF
  • 1982

    Thermoplastic aromatic polyetherketones — PEEK

    John B. Rose & Philip A. Staniland (ICI) · US 4,320,224 (priority 1977)

    The polyetheretherketone patent — the summit of high-performance thermoplastics, now a star material in both technical molding and high-temperature additive manufacturing.

    PatentPDF
  • 1986

    Apparatus for production of three-dimensional objects by stereolithography

    Charles W. Hull (3D Systems) · US 4,575,330

    The founding patent of additive manufacturing — layer-by-layer photopolymerization, the origin of 3D Systems and of the STL file format.

    PatentPDF
  • 1992

    Apparatus and method for creating three-dimensional objects — FDM

    S. Scott Crump (Stratasys) · US 5,121,329

    Fused deposition modeling: molten filament extrusion, layer upon layer. Its expiry in 2009 triggered the desktop 3D-printing explosion.

    PatentPDF

citation format: Inventor, A. (Year). Title (US Patent No. X). USPTO. Google Patents URL
